Visualizing the Housing Crisis

This project was part of a BCIT cartography course and involved designing a thematic map with the freedom of choosing the geographic area, map theme, data, feature classes, and specifications like symbology, data classification, colours, symbols, annotation, etc.

While brainstorming ideas for this project I knew I wanted to do something with census data. The issue of housing affordability is rather prominent and seemed like it would make an interesting theme. Below, as you can see, I used dissemination areas (which are the smallest geographic areas for census data) for the main map and census tracts for the key map, and chose to display the particular metric of percentage of households that spend more than 30% of their income on shelter costs which includes both renters and owners.

An interesting feature of this dataset is how Stanley Park shows up in the 40-50% range. Within that area there are some residential buildings but they are located next to adjacent census areas which gives a false impression that the entire park is populated with houses. I decided to leave Stanley Park in the final dataset for the sake of accurately representing the source data. Though if I were to do it again I think I would exclude Stanley Park or modify the polygon to overlap with the real locations of shelters within that area. Also, green coloured roads might seem like an odd choice but they contrast well with the orange/red polygons

Although much of the areas are lightly coloured, indicating that only 10-30% of households are affected, that still seems like a rather significant proportion which comes at no surprise given the reputation Vancouver has with respect to housing affordability. It will be interesting to see how data from the next census compares. However, I suspect it will show a worsening trend.
